CSM Alive

The Jellyfish Light Show is the name of the CSM Alive performance I produced and created with Inigo Townsend. The original stimulus for this project was came from the sight of my seeing hundreds of various breeds of jellyfish washed up dead on the seashore first thing in the morning when I was back home in West Wales over the first coronavirus lockdown in June 2020. This triggered feelings of being washed up myself, something I wanted to capture in this final piece. At the same time, I wanted to create something fun to evoke and my pre-pandemic life. Inigo and I were imagining the atmosphere of the piece and how we could best describe what happened to these jellyfish in the night leading up to them washing up dead on the shore.



Inigo and I visited the Electronica exhibition at the Design Museum in November 2020 and this reminded us how much we'd been missing electronic music and lifestyle. This led us onto the path of electronic music and nightlife, something we had both been missing.



The Jellyfish Light Show is a dance movement piece choreographed in collaboration with the three performers: Stella, Charles, and Isobel. This 13-minute performance was performed in the Studio Theatre at Central Saint Martins on May 19th, 2021.

In creating this performance, Inigo and I divided the design work between the two of us: I drew the costumes and Inigo drew the set. We agreed what we wanted to achieve.

Together, we knew that for that for the set we wanted to use live feed cameras with projections onto screens as we had done Design for Dance performance the previous year, and for our collective project: Just Another Soup Can.

December 18th, 2020. Experiment in PDP studio.



In the early days of designing the Jellyfish Light Show, Inigo and I worked out a timeline of the piece including key moment that we wanted to create, defining mood change or disorientation. In this timeline we included the lighting design that we imagined going with each mood change. This timeline breakdown was used to show to the sound composer, Significant Other, and the dancers when we presented the project to them.




I use my sketchbook in the design process to try out different shapes on the body. This is important to develop on my understanding what will work on stage during the performance. Through research into the shapes and textures of sea creatures, I found secondary references of Dutch fashion designer Iris Van Herpen haute couture collection. From using these references in my sketchbook, I can imagine different aspects of the costume to figure out if they will work for a movement piece. For example, at this point early in the design process I wanted to use knee high socks with gathers to represent the jellyfish tentacles going down the body. I didn’t use knee socks, I ended up using this concept to go all over the body on the blue costume for ‘Glacial’.



The Pepper’s Ghost technique was introduced to us by the theatre company Imitating the Dog and in lessons taught by Pete Brooks. Inigo and I were inspired by the concept for (RE) presenting reality to the audience in front of them by augmenting the image to create another dimension to the atmosphere of the room. The below image is from an experimental day in the studio theatre on March 24th 2021.



We decided to use three performers to give the impression of a cluster of jellyfish, similar to the ones I had seen on the sands. In terms of costumes, from a previous dance project I knew that the stretchiness of lycra leotards and leggings would be ideal to allow the dancers maximum movement. To achieve a strong tactile effect, I chose velvet, organza and taffeta to sculpt the shapes of ocean creatures, including the frill effect of jellyfish tentacles.



Covid restrictions proved especially challenging for several reasons during the creation of the production. Having to source fabrics for the costumes online, for example, meant I was unable to see and feel them. Social distancing also made pattern cutting more difficult without the costume technicians help to make the adjustments onto paper patterns to fit the dancers, most importantly not being able to work closely with the dancers during the costume making. We also knew that because of restrictions, the possibility of the final performance having to be in an outdoor space would have a huge impact on lighting and the final visual effect. Many final decisions, therefore, had to be made in a short space of time. The way that we choreographed the piece meant that we could collaborate with the performers to make a 10-minute show. Inigo and I focused on two key moments in the music to achieve intensity in the movement. This included a build up of slow bobbing movements in synchronicity that led to each of the performer disconnected in an overwhelming shaking movement.


Through the experimentation process I came up with three options for how I could make the puff’s for the arms and legs of the character Swelter. All three samples have different qualities and out if these three, the top and bottom ones shown in the image were the best. Still, they were not exactly what I wanted because the top sample was too curly to sew each individual strip of fabric with shirring elastic. Whereas the bottom sample was better structured and more durable, it lost all of its texture. Therefore I decided to continue sampling until I made a puff that would be durable and textured.


After spending much time experimenting with various colours of organza, using several techniques. I finally chose to create the 16 puffs for the costume of swelter using the technique called ‘knife pleats’. During this time I also finished all of the alterations for the unitard, meaning I could attach the puffs directly onto the arms and legs.

I brought the work in progress to rehearsal so that I could see how the fabrics looked under the lights in the Studio Theatre. This image shows Charles warming up with the part finished costume on.




Every week on Tuesdays and Thursdays, Inigo and I would rehearse with the dancers Stella, Isobel and Charles. This meant that we were able to collaborate on the choreography to tell the story of the jellyfish.
